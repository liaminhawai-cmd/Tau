Tau  ·  Strategic-depth analysis

The evidence points to a genuinely deep game.

We ran 1,280 computer-versus-computer games across eight strength levels, each starting from a slightly different position, to answer one question a strong abstract game has to pass: as the players get better, does the game collapse onto a single winning recipe, or does skilled play stay varied?

Verdict:  it stays varied at every level, and no forced win was found.

Produced with Claude, Anthropic’s AI assistant, running the game’s own engine. This is an AI analysis, not an independent human study.

The headline question

Strong play stays varied. It never narrows to one line.

The clearest warning sign in an abstract game is convergence: once players are good enough, everyone plays the same handful of moves and the game feels solved. To test for it we had each strength level play itself, 24 times, from tiny random starting nudges, and looked at how many genuinely different opening lines came out (counting which foot pivots and which way it turns, ignoring exact angles).

A solved or degenerate game would land near the bottom of the chart: one or two lines, repeated over and over. Tau does the opposite. Even the strongest level, playing itself, produces a dozen distinct opening lines across 24 games, and its single most-common line shows up in only about 1 in 5. Skilled play does focus a little as strength rises, exactly as it does in chess or Go, but it stays a long way from a single forced line.

Comeback potential

Being ahead helps. It rarely settles the game.

Depth also lives in whether a lead is safe. We pooled every position from all 1,280 games and measured, for a given size of lead, how often the player who was ahead actually went on to win. If a small edge locked in the result, the game would be shallow.

It does not. From a dead-even position the eventual winner is a coin flip (47%). A real, visible lead is worth only about 75%, so better than one game in five is still lost from there. It takes a commanding lead to reach ~85%, and even that is not certain. In practice the outcome almost never becomes a foregone conclusion: only 0.9% of games reached a point where the result was locked in before the closing moves.

Fairness

Going first is a small edge at most, never a decisive one.

Many simple games are quietly settled by who moves first. Tau is not one of them. Across all 1,280 games the first player wins exactly 50%; in level-versus-itself play it averages 41%, if anything a slight edge to the responder. The edge does vary by level, and at the very top there is a mild first-mover advantage of roughly two-thirds. That is the ordinary, healthy edge strong abstract games carry; chess, Go and Hex all favour the opener a little. What matters is that nowhere, at any level, does it come near the near-certain win a forced opening would produce.

The strength range

The eight levels are a real ladder, not window dressing.

For the analysis above to mean anything, the eight levels have to actually differ in skill and span a wide range. They do. The strongest levels beat the entire field below them cleanly, so the "does it converge at the top?" question really is being asked at the top of a genuine ladder, not among near-equal opponents. Lower down it gets more interesting: the aggressive early levels can punish the cautious middle ones, so skill here is not a single straight line. Style beating style is itself a mark of a game with more than one good idea in it.

Shape of a game

Games have an arc, and they vary.

Finally, the games are not all the same length or over in a flash. A typical game runs 17 turns to someone going off the edge, with plenty of short skirmishes and long grinds on either side. Under 3% ran long enough to hit our safety cut-off. That spread is what you want: room for different kinds of games rather than one rhythm on repeat.

We checked whether that cut-off was hiding true stalemates, and it is not. Of the games that reached it, only a handful were genuine deadlocks with neither side making progress: about 0.3% of all games. The rest had a clear leader and simply had not landed the finishing push yet. The few real grinds nearly all came from one very defensive strength level playing itself, the matchup most prone to it. When we raised the cut-off to 300 turns and replayed those matchups, they resolved: the two strongest levels ended every game, and even that grinding matchup finished nine in ten. Tau has slow positions, not stalemated ones.

How this was measured

Method, in plain terms.

Everything here comes from the game's real engine playing against itself, not a simplified model. The same code that runs the game ran the tournament.

At a glance

Games played
1,280 full games (every pairing of 8 levels, both colours first, 20 games each) plus 72 self-play games for the opening-variety test.
Players
The eight built-in difficulty levels, from a gentle beginner to the engine's strongest setting.
Starting positions
Each game began from the standard setup with a small random nudge to both pieces, so no two games were forced to be identical.
What "a lead" means
How much closer to safety one player's nearest-the-edge piece sits than the other's. The win-chance curve is measured, not assumed.
Honest caveats
This is the computer opponents playing themselves. Strong self-play is good evidence of depth but not a substitute for a human tournament; a fuller picture would add human games over time. The opening-variety counts use a modest sample per level.

Technical appendix

The detail, for readers who want it.

Everything above stands on its own. This appendix is for the technically inclined: how the eight computer opponents actually work and how they differ, the exact parameters, the full result tables, and a stress test that pushed the strongest brain harder still. None of it is needed to read the report, and none of it changes the conclusion.

The engine and the tournament

Every game was played by the shipping game's own code, not a re-implementation or a simplified model. A turn is driven synchronously: the active brain returns a plan, the piece is rotated in 3-degree steps until it reaches that plan's stop angle or the rules halt it, and the turn ends. The physics is deterministic, so a chosen plan replays to exactly the position the brain searched.

To stop the near-deterministic top brains from playing the identical game every time, each game began from the standard start with an independent random rotation of up to ±6° applied to each piece. That keeps every position a real, legal Tau position while giving genuinely different lines. Games ran to a piece leaving the board, or to a 70-turn safety cap (reached in 2.9% of games), after which the piece with the better rim margin was recorded as ahead.

The full tournament was every ordered pairing of the eight levels (64 pairings, both colours getting the first move), 20 games each, for 1,280 games. A separate 72-game run (24 self-play games at three levels) measured opening variety, and the stress test below added more.

The eight brains, one by one

Six of the eight share one planner. A move is a triple: which of the three feet is pinned, which way it rotates, and the stop angle. The planner sweeps each of the six (pivot × direction) options in 3° steps until the one-crossing rule or a 170° safety cap stops it, scores candidate stopping points with a weighted evaluation of the resulting position, and, on the deeper rungs, searches the opponent's reply and its own follow-up. Two brains are deliberate exceptions. Elo figures come from a roughly 600-game round-robin; each rung is tuned to beat the one below it about 60 to 65% of the time.

L1 · Pushover Elo 0 (baseline)

kind: l1

Pins the foot nearest the opponent, swings the farthest foot toward them, but stops at a random 45 to 80% of the legal limit and often halts before even the first crossing. One move in five picks a random pivot instead. It gives no thought to its own safety. Built to be beatable by a complete first-timer.

L2 · Menu “Easy” Elo ~117

kind: builtin · dumb heuristic · no lookahead, no evaluation

One fixed rule. If it sits closer to the centre than the opponent it attacks (pin the foot second-farthest from the opponent, swing the farthest foot toward them); otherwise it defends with the same shape aimed at the centre. It always stops a random 60 to 90% short of the natural limit, so it never fully commits.

L3 · Cautious pusher Elo ~200

kind: p · 1-ply · sampleDeg 26 · keepStops 1 · w: margin .6 · noise .30

The first brain on the shared planner: one ply, coarse 26° sampling, scored on rim safety alone. A weakness dial makes it play a purely random legal move 30% of the time. Diluting a competent brain this way reads as more human than a brain that simply under-commits.

L4 · Centre-dancer Elo ~273

kind: p · 1-ply · sampleDeg 18 · keepStops 2 · w: margin .5, zone .3, center 1.5

Finer 18° sampling, still one ply, but now valuing board control: rim safety, the hand-graded cell values, and a strong bonus for keeping a foot near the middle. That centre bonus is what produces its dramatic full-board swings.

L5 · Corner-cutter Elo ~323

kind: p · 1-ply · sampleDeg 18 · keepStops 2 · w: margin .6, zone .4, park 2

Adds the park term: a bonus for resting a foot on a line near a corner intersection, arming it to cut a second line next turn. It begins setting up the two-lines-for-one-crossing trick, though it still does not look at the reply.

L6 · Solid Elo ~392

kind: p · 2-ply · sampleDeg 24 · keepStops 1 · maxCands 8 · w: margin 1

The first brain that looks ahead. For each sampled move it lets the opponent answer with their best sweep-to-limit reply and scores by the worst case (minimax). Pure rim safety, but now defended against the punishing counter that a one-ply brain walks into.

L7 · Expert Elo ~461

kind: builtin · master · 3-ply minimax

Full three-ply search: its move, the opponent's best reply, then its own best follow-up, so it will not take a move that looks good now but walks into a bad answer. Its move set adds early park stops that deliberately arm a foot in a line's grace band, letting the deeper plies see and race the double-cross. Evaluation is margin plus zone plus a park bonus, with the win/loss term dominating. Alpha-beta-style cutoffs keep it quick.

L8 · Cap Elo ~550 (Expert +90, engine ceiling)

kind: p3 · 3-ply · sampleDeg 18 · keepStops 2 · maxCands 11 · w: margin 1, zone .5, park .9, center .15

Expert's three-ply shape, but the root is built from fine 18° partial-rotation stops rather than only sweep-to-limit moves, so it weighs stopping points Expert never considers, under the fullest weight set (all four positional terms). This is the strongest opponent the game ships.

What the brains value

Positions are scored by a weighted sum of these terms. Weights are the per-level dials listed above; a winning or losing position dominates every positional term.

margin
Rim safety: the opponent's outermost foot clearance from the edge minus mine. Positive means the opponent is nearer to falling. This is the core objective and it decides close calls.
zone
Hand-graded cell value: 4 in the centre, 3 mid, 2 outer, minus 1 inside either corner lens pocket. Summed over my three feet minus the opponent's.
center
Bonus for keeping a foot near the board's middle, which drives large board-crossing swings.
park
Bonus per foot resting in a line's grace band near an intersection, ready to cross a second line next turn (the double-cross setup).
noise
Probability of ignoring the evaluation entirely and playing a random legal move. The weakness dial for the lower rungs.

The deeper-search stress test (L8+)

The one worry the main report cannot fully close is subtle: what if the game only looks varied because even L8 is not strong enough to find a hidden best line, and a deeper search would collapse onto it? To probe that, we built L8+: the exact same evaluation as L8 (identical weights, identical judgement) given a much wider, finer search: half the sample spacing, double the kept stops, and roughly 2.5 times as many candidate lines searched in full. This is more thinking time, not a different player.

First we confirmed the extra search actually makes it stronger, so the test is meaningful: L8+ beat stock L8 in 16 of 24 head-to-head games (67%). It is a genuinely better player, not just a different one. Then the two depth questions:

Opening variety went up, not down. Playing itself from perturbed starts, L8+ produced 17 distinct opening lines out of 24 games with its single most-common line appearing only 8% of the time, more varied than stock L8 (12 of 24, top line 21%). A deeper search found more good ideas, not one forced line.

No forced line at any depth. Playing itself 30 times from perturbed starts, L8+ produced 30 distinct games: no full move sequence ever repeated another. Its median game ran 21 turns. Going first was worth about two-thirds here (20 of 30), the same mild first-mover edge stock L8 shows at the top of the ladder, and nowhere near a forced win.

This is the strongest single piece of evidence in the report. The exact failure a publisher would fear, that strength hides a degenerate line, is where the game held up best: give the top brain far more search and it fans out rather than narrowing.

Termination: does the cut-off hide draws?

Games in the main tournament ran to a piece leaving the board or a 70-turn cap, and 2.9% hit the cap. A fair worry is that the cap converts genuine stalemates into recorded wins. We checked two ways.

First, we classified all 37 capped games by their margin trajectory. Only 4 (0.3% of all 1,280 games) were near-even deadlocks with neither side progressing. The median capped game held a rim-margin lead of 15, a clear leader that simply had not delivered the final push. And 22 of the 37 were a level playing itself, 10 of those the pure-defence L6 against its clone, exactly the matchup that should grind.

Definitions and honest limits

perturbation
The ±6° per-piece rotation applied at the start of every game so the near-deterministic brains do not replay one fixed line.
fingerprint
An FNV-1a hash of a game's full move sequence (pivot, direction, stop angle to the nearest degree). Two games sharing a fingerprint played literally identically, the strongest possible signal of a forced line.
point of no return
The first turn after which the eventual winner's calibrated win chance stays at or above 90% to the end. It exists in only 0.86% of games (11 of 1,280).
calibration
The measured, not assumed, map from lead size to win chance, pooled over every position in every decided game.

What this is and is not. Strong self-play is good evidence of depth, but it is the computer opponents playing themselves, not a human tournament. A fuller picture would add human games over time. The calibration pools positions across games and turns, so its buckets are not independent samples; it is a descriptive curve, not a fitted model. The opening-variety counts use a modest 24-game sample per level. And because Tau's state is continuous (any stop angle is legal), no amount of self-play can prove no forced win exists; it can only fail to find one across a wide, strong search, which is what happened here. The reproducible artifacts are the 1,280-game record, the analysis scripts, and the deeper-search run described above.

Tau

A new class of board game.

Innovation usually begins not by adding to what already exists, but by removing an established rule or convention. Most abstract strategy games, checkers, chess, Go, have a few things in common: several pieces to coordinate, pieces that move along straight lines, and moves made in discrete steps, no fraction of a move allowed. Tau is what’s left when you take all three away.

Each player has a single three-legged piece on a circular board. The object is to push your opponent’s piece off the edge, using your own. Each turn one leg is kept in place while the other two swing around it in an arc. That movement is continuous, and while there’s a limit to how far you can go, you can stop at any point before it. When the pieces collide they move in regular but intrinsically complex ways, out of the geometry and weight of the piece. It rewards spatial thinking and precision over calculation.

A third kind of complexity

All competitive board games are a contest over who best grapples with complexity, and they mostly get there two ways. Chess is complicated: a complicated assortment of simple pieces and rules, making complex tactical problems. Go is emergent: simple rules and pieces, with the complexity appearing at scale.

Tau is reaching for a third kind. When two people grapple, their moves can’t be recorded and repeated precisely. They aren’t analysing long sequences; they’re dealing with an opponent that is fundamentally complicated, using a different style of judgement to decide. Call it irreducible. Tau distils that down to a simple set of rules, takes out the strength and the dexterity, and tries to keep some of the irreducible complexity of the interaction.
